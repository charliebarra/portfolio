Design Notebook

Short thoughts on games, systems, and why players do weird things.

These are brief observations, not finished essays. They help me keep track of questions I want to explore through future projects.

Why randomness can make strategy better

Randomness is fun when it creates uncertainty, but frustrating when it removes agency. The best luck gives players something new to respond to.

What Pokémon taught me about balance

A metagame never stays still. When players solve one strategy, new counters appear.

Players optimize the fun out of games

If the most effective strategy is boring, many players will still use it. The system has to make the strong choice interesting too.

Probability is not the same as fairness

A result can be statistically reasonable and still feel unfair if the player had no way to prepare or respond.

The moment players broke my game

Playtesting Meteor Mayhem showed me strategies I had never considered. That was useful, even when it meant changing the rules.

Good UI should almost disappear

The player should be thinking about the decision, not searching for the button that lets them make it.

A tutorial is part of the design

Teaching the rules is not separate from the game. It shapes the player's first experience with the system.

Constraints can improve ideas

Limits force me to decide which mechanics actually matter.

The difference between difficult and confusing

A hard choice can be satisfying. Not understanding what the game wants is usually just frustrating.

Why small changes matter

One number, rule, or card frequency can affect several connected parts of a system.

Players need room to surprise you

A system becomes more interesting when it supports strategies the designer did not prescribe.

Build the smallest test first

A rough prototype can answer a design question faster than polishing an idea that has not been tested.

Charlie Barra

Curious about systems. Passionate about building better ones.
