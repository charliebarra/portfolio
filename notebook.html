Design notebook

Short thoughts on games, systems, and why players do weird things.

These are not formal essays. They are the kinds of questions I keep coming back to while building, testing, playing, and trying to understand why some systems feel better than others.

01

Why randomness can make strategy better

Randomness is frustrating when it decides the game for you. It is interesting when it creates a problem you still have agency to solve. I like games where luck changes the situation, but the player still has to make the best decision from that new position.

02

What competitive Pokémon taught me about balance

A metagame is never finished. When one strategy becomes popular, players build counters. Then the counters create new problems. That cycle made me realize balance is not just a spreadsheet. It is also how real players respond to each other.

03

Players optimize fun out of games

If a game rewards one strategy too strongly, players will use it even if it makes the game less fun. That is not really the player’s fault. It means the system is telling them what to do. Good design should leave room for smart choices without making experimentation feel like a mistake.

04

Simple rules can create complicated choices

I like games where the rules are easy to explain but the decisions are hard. That is harder to design than it sounds. Adding more rules is easy. Making a small rule interact with other systems in interesting ways is much harder.

05

Roblox changed how I think about creators

Roblox showed me that players do not just consume games. They build inside them, remix ideas, and create their own goals. That made me more interested in designing systems that give players room to shape the experience instead of just following one path.

06

Probability is not the same as fairness

Something can be statistically fair and still feel unfair. If a player loses to an effect they could not predict, prepare for, or respond to, the math might be balanced but the experience still feels bad. That was important while balancing Meteor Mayhem.

07

The moment players broke my game

Playtesting is awkward because people immediately find things you missed. But that is the point. If players misunderstand a rule or discover a strategy that ruins the pacing, the design is giving you useful information.

08

Good UI should almost disappear

When an interface works well, players stop thinking about the interface and focus on decisions. When it fails, the interface becomes the game in the worst way. I notice this a lot in games with confusing menus or unclear feedback.

09

Cybersecurity also feels like systems design

Cybersecurity made me think about systems from the opposite direction: how they break, where they are vulnerable, and how small decisions can create big problems. That connects more to game design than I expected.

10

Why feedback loops matter

Players need to understand what happened, why it happened, and what they can do next. Without feedback, a system feels random even when it is not. Clear feedback makes learning possible.

11

Easy to learn, hard to master

This phrase gets used a lot, but I think it means a system should welcome new players while still rewarding deeper understanding. The first game should make sense. The tenth game should still surprise you.

12

Why I like building prototypes

A prototype does not have to be pretty. It just has to answer a question. Does this mechanic work? Do players understand it? Is the decision interesting? I learn more from a rough playable version than from keeping an idea perfect in my head.
