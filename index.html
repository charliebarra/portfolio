Case study

Meteor Mayhem: designing a complete strategy system

Meteor Mayhem was Charlie’s first full attempt to design and complete a strategy game from concept to playable prototype.

Project snapshot

Role: Lead Designer & Creator

Focus: balance, resource management, decision-making

Format: multiplayer strategy card game

Tools: prototyping, playtesting, card layout, 3D/physical components

Design goal

Create a competitive card game where players balance risk vs. reward, resource collection, timing, ship upgrades, and defensive strategy. The winner is determined by the total value of collected resources at the end of the game.

Process

The project moved through research, ideation, mechanic brainstorming, early prototypes, testing, card balancing, and reflection. The most important lesson: players never behave exactly how a designer expects them to.

Balancing the system

The project included defined card categories, tiered resources, ship capacity, mining tiers, tools, upgrades, events, and disasters. Charlie refined the game through systematic testing and modeled win probabilities based on deck composition and draw outcomes.

Reflection

Playtesting with friends and the local gaming community helped Charlie improve the game’s balance and better understand how players interact with systems differently than expected during development.

Design notebook

What I’m thinking about

Short reflections that can become blog-style portfolio entries later.

Why randomness can make strategy better

Randomness is fun when it creates uncertainty, but frustrating when it removes agency. The hard part is designing luck that players can prepare for, respond to, and sometimes use creatively.

What Pokémon taught me about balance

A metagame is never static. As players solve one strategy, new counters emerge. That cycle helped me understand that balance is not just math—it is also how real players adapt.

How Roblox changed how I think

Roblox introduced me to the idea that players could also become creators. I became interested in how simple mechanics and player-driven systems can create completely different experiences.
