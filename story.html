Story • 4 minute read

Curious about systems. Passionate about building better ones.

Every designer starts somewhere. Looking back, I can see that each experience changed the kinds of questions I was asking. Over time, I stopped focusing only on how to play games and became more interested in how they were built.

This is not a timeline of everything I have done. It is the story of how my interests shifted from playing competitive systems, to studying them, to trying to design my own.

Chapter One

Age 8 • 2018

Discovering Strategy

Pokémon was not just the first competitive game I loved. It was the first time I realized that games could reward planning, prediction, and creative thinking instead of just fast reactions. Every match felt like a puzzle, and I wanted to understand why certain strategies worked better than others.

Some players succeeded by making bold predictions. Others relied on consistency and careful planning. That variety made the same game feel different every time.

Great games reward thoughtful decisions, not just quick reactions.
Chapter Two

Age 9 • 2019

Discovering Competition

Traveling to my first major tournaments showed me that games create communities just as much as they create competition. I met players from different places who all approached the same game in completely different ways.

Winning mattered, but learning from other players mattered just as much. I started to see competitive games as living systems that change because players keep solving them.

There is never just one way to solve a problem.
Chapter Three

Age 10 • 2020

Learning from Setbacks

Qualifying for the Pokémon World Championships was one of the most exciting moments I had experienced. I was preparing for London when the tournament was cancelled because of the COVID-19 pandemic.

At the time, it was disappointing. Looking back, it taught me something important: progress is not defined by one event. Even when plans change unexpectedly, learning continues.

Keep building, even when the path changes.
Chapter Four

Ages 11–12 • 2021–2022

Becoming Curious

When competitive play returned, I noticed that something had changed. I still wanted to compete, but I had started asking different questions.

Instead of only wondering how to win, I became interested in why certain strategies dominated, how game balance evolved over time, and how small rule changes could reshape the way people played.

Curiosity can become its own kind of competition.
Chapter Five

Ages 13–14 • 2023–2024

From Player to Builder

Roblox introduced me to an idea I had not really considered before: players could become creators. Designing worlds, experimenting with mechanics, and imagining different player experiences made me realize I enjoyed building systems as much as interacting with them.

Around the same time, cybersecurity and programming introduced me to another kind of system, one built from logic instead of game mechanics. The fields were different, but I kept asking the same kinds of questions: Why does this work? How do small changes affect the whole system? How can something be both intuitive and resilient?

The best way to understand a system is to build one.
Chapter Six

Age 15 • 2025

Designing My Own Game

Meteor Mayhem was the first time I brought together everything I had been learning about strategy, balance, probability, and player decision-making into a project of my own.

Designing the game taught me that ideas rarely work perfectly the first time. Playtesting became one of the most valuable parts of the process because players constantly surprised me. Sometimes they discovered strategies I had not considered. Other times they were confused by mechanics that seemed obvious to me.

Iteration is where ideas become experiences.
Chapter Seven

Age 16 • Today

Just Getting Started

Today I am exploring Python development, probability modeling, and interactive learning experiences while preparing to study game design in college.

Every project leaves me with more questions than answers, and that is one of the things I enjoy most about design. Whether I am building a game, writing code, or exploring a new idea, I am interested in how systems influence the choices people make and how thoughtful design can make those experiences more meaningful.

Curious about systems. Passionate about building better ones.

Continue exploring.

Every project in this portfolio represents another step in that journey. Some began as questions. Others began as experiments. All of them helped me better understand how thoughtful design shapes the way people think, learn, and play.
