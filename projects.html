Flagship case study • 2025

Meteor Mayhem

My first attempt at designing an entire strategic system from scratch: rules, cards, resources, upgrades, hazards, playtesting, and balance.

RoleLead Designer & Creator
FormatStrategy Card Game
StatusPlayable Prototype
FocusBalance + Systems

Design challenge

Can luck matter without deciding who wins?

Meteor Mayhem started with a simple problem that turned out to be complicated: I wanted the game to feel unpredictable without making players feel powerless. Players needed to collect resources, upgrade ships, use events, defend against hazards, and make choices that mattered even when the card draw changed the situation.

I learned that balance is not just making every card mathematically equal. Balance is also how players feel when a card changes the game.

Design goals

Meaningful choices

Players should have more than one reasonable path to winning.

Risk vs. reward

Better resources should be tempting, but not automatic.

Player interaction

Events and disasters should create tension without ruining the game.

Replayability

Different draws and decisions should create different outcomes.

Readable rules

Players should spend more time making choices than decoding cards.

Balance

No single strategy should feel obviously dominant every time.

System breakdown

Meteor Mayhem is built from interacting systems.

Resource System

Players collect resources with different values, so timing and risk matter.

Upgrade System

Ship upgrades change capacity, mining strength, and long-term strategy.

Tool System

Tools create tactical choices like protection, stealing, recycling, and extra actions.

Event System

Events disrupt the game through swaps, trades, blockades, and surprise effects.

Disaster System

Hazards add pressure and stop the game from becoming one perfect plan.

Scoring System

The winner is based on total resource value, not just who collected the most cards.

Card design + UX

Readable cards, clear categories, fast decisions.

I used color, icons, short text, and consistent layouts so players could quickly understand what each card was meant to do. The goal was to keep the focus on decisions, not on rereading rules every turn.

Testing + iteration

What changed after testing

Players never behave exactly how you expect.

Watching people play helped me find confusing rules, overpowered effects, pacing problems, and moments where the game became more interesting than I expected. It also made me more comfortable changing ideas I originally liked.

Reflection

What this project taught me about design.

Iteration beats the first idea.

The game improved most when I was willing to change or remove mechanics that were not working.

Balance is felt by players.

A card can look mathematically fair but still feel annoying or too punishing during play.

Feedback creates better systems.

Testing with other players showed me interactions I never would have found alone.

Roblox design studies

Roblox introduced me to the idea that players can also become creators.

I became interested in how simple mechanics and player-driven systems can create completely different gameplay experiences depending on how communities interact with them.
